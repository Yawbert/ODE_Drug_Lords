Engineering Problem

Forecasting the Fate of Spilled Oil

When an oil spill occurs at sea, a series of simultaneous, interdependent physical and chemical processes take place. For environmental engineers and marine response teams, accurately forecasting both short-term and long-term behaviour of spilled oil is essential for designing effective clean-up strategies and mitigating ecological damage.


The core engineering challenge is that all physical phenomena occur simultaneously and are nonlinearly coupled. An engineering solution must treat these processes as a coupled system rather than computing each one independently.


This project formulates all five governing processes as a system of coupled, nonlinear ordinary differential equations (ODEs), then implements and compares numerical solvers to produce quantitative predictions of the oil's state over time.

The oil spill process

Governing Processes
  • 01
    Spreading

    Spreading is the expansion of the oil slick over the sea surface after the spill. In the early stage, it is mainly controlled by gravity, surface tension, oil volume, density difference between oil and seawater, and viscosity. The article explains that spreading is important because it affects other processes: a larger slick area increases evaporation and influences dispersion.

  • 02
    Evaporation

    Evaporation is the loss of lighter hydrocarbon components from the oil into the atmosphere. The article identifies it as one of the most important early mass-loss processes, especially during the first hours to first 24 hours. As lighter fractions evaporate, the remaining oil becomes heavier, denser, and more viscous.

  • 03
    Emulsification

    Emulsification occurs when water droplets become mixed into the oil, forming a water-in-oil mixture often called mousse. According to the article, this can greatly increase the volume of the slick and cause a major rise in viscosity. The process depends on oil composition, especially components such as asphaltenes and waxes, as well as environmental mixing.

  • 04
    Volume variation / Dispersion

    Entrainment or natural dispersion happens when oil is broken into droplets and mixed into the water column, especially under wind and wave action. This reduces the amount of oil remaining on the surface. The article treats this as important because it competes with evaporation: oil that enters the water column is no longer available to evaporate from the surface slick.

  • 05
    Viscosity & Density Change

    Viscosity and density change as the oil weathers. Evaporation removes lighter components, making the oil denser and more viscous, while emulsification adds water and further increases both density and viscosity. These changes matter because thicker, heavier oil spreads less easily, disperses differently, and becomes harder to recover or treat.

Mathematical Model

The Coupled ODE System

Each process is described by a differential equation. Because the state variables depend on each other, the five equations form a nonlinearly coupled system solved simultaneously at each time step.

Author's Modification

The authors adapted existing oil spill models by taking equations from previous studies and rewriting them so they could work together in one coupled numerical system.

For spreading, they used a Fay-type spreading model, which was later modified by researchers such as Wang, Lehr, Mackay, Reed, and Spaulding, and expressed it in a differential form suitable for numerical simulation.

For evaporation, they used the Stiver and Mackay method, which relates evaporation to factors such as wind speed, temperature, slick thickness, and oil properties. This was also rewritten as a differential equation so it could be included in the system.

For emulsification, they applied Mackay’s water incorporation model and converted it into a time-dependent form. Density and viscosity were modelled using empirical relationships that depend on evaporation and water content.

Overall, the main contribution of the authors was combining these separate models into one coupled system of nonlinear ordinary differential equations, which was then solved using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method.

EQ 01 Evaporation Rate

This equation describes how the fraction of oil evaporated (FE) changes over time in an oil spill. The rate (d FE /dt) depends on environmental conditions and oil properties: higher temperature (T) increases evaporation through the exponential term (exp (A - BT)), while wind and mixing effects are represented by the mass transfer coefficient (K). The spill surface area (A) and initial volume (V0) control how much oil is exposed and available to evaporate. The constants (T0, TG, A, and B), derived from distillation data, describe how the crude oil composition changes during evaporation. The term (T0+TG FE) shows that as more oil evaporates, the remaining oil becomes heavier and evaporates more slowly, meaning evaporation is fast initially but gradually decreases over time.

$$ \frac{dF_E}{dt} = \left( \frac{K \cdot A}{V_o} \right) \cdot \exp\left( A - \left( \frac{B}{T} \right) \cdot (T_o + T_G \cdot F_E) \right) $$
FE fraction evaporated K mass transfer coefficient A slick area (m²) V₀ initial spill volume T₀, TG, A, B distillation constants T temperature (K)
EQ 02 Volume Variation Rate

The rate of change in oil volume on the sea surface shows that it decreases due to two main processes: evaporation and entrainment. The first term represents the loss of oil as lighter components evaporate into the atmosphere. The second term represents entrainment, where a fraction of the remaining oil is mixed into the water column by waves and turbulence. The equation shows that the oil slick continuously shrinks on the surface due to both evaporation and physical removal into the sea.

$$ \frac{dV}{dt} = -V_0 \cdot \left( \frac{dF_E}{dt} \right) - D \cdot V $$
V surface volume (m³) D volume variation fraction per hour dFE/dt evaporation rate (from EQ 01)
EQ 03 Emulsification (Water Uptake)

Water is incorporated into the oil driven by wind energy, forming a stable water-in-oil emulsion called "mousse". The rate depends on wind speed W and slows as the water content Y approaches its maximum C₃ (≈ 0.7 for crude oils). This is the only equation with an exact closed-form analytical solution.

$$ \frac{dY}{dt} = 2.0 \times 10^{-6} \cdot (W + 1)^2 \cdot \left( 1 - \frac{Y}{C_3} \right) $$
Y fractional water content W wind speed (m/s) C₃ max water content ≈ 0.7
EQ 04 Spreading Area

The spreading mechanism describes how an oil slick expands on the sea surface after a spill due to gravity, surface tension, and viscosity, provided the oil’s pour point is below seawater temperature, so it remains liquid. It depends on variables such as spill volume (V), slick area (A), density difference (Δρ), and water viscosity (νw). In Fay’s model, larger spills spread faster at first, but the rate slows as the slick area increases, with wind further enhancing spreading.

$$ \frac{dA}{dt} = K_1 \cdot A^{-1} \cdot V^{4/3} $$
A slick area (m²) V current spill volume K₁ spreading constant (default 150 s⁻¹)
EQ 05 Viscosity Change

The ODE describes how the viscosity of spilt oil changes over time due to two coupled processes: evaporation and emulsification. The first term shows that as the fraction evaporated increases, light components are lost, making the oil thicker. The second term represents emulsification, where increasing water content (Y) from wind and waves forms a ‘mousse’ also increasing viscosity but limited by the factor (1−C3Y)2. Overall, the equation shows that viscosity grows over time as both evaporation and water uptake act together, making the oil progressively thicker and harder to disperse.

$$ \frac{d\mu}{dt} = C_4 \cdot \mu \cdot \left( \frac{dF_E}{dt} \right) + \frac{2.5 \cdot \mu}{(1 - C_3 \cdot Y)^2} \cdot \left( \frac{dY}{dt} \right) $$
μ dynamic viscosity (cP) C₄ evaporation–viscosity coefficient dFE/dt from EQ 01 dY/dt from EQ 03
Analytical Solution Attempt

Symbolic Analysis with MATLAB dsolve

Each ODE was passed individually to MATLAB's symbolic toolbox to determine whether a closed-form analytical solution exists. Equilibrium and steady-state analysis was also performed. Only the ODE for the water content Y yielded an actual closed-form solution. The others gave incomplete ones. The results confirm the necessity of numerical methods.

Implicit integral form
EQ 01 — Evaporation (FE)

dsolve returned an implicit integral expression because the exponential term contains A(t), which is itself governed by a separate nonlinear ODE. Full decoupling is not possible.

Integrating-factor form
EQ 02 — Volume (V)

An integrating-factor solution dependent on FE(t) was returned. Because FE(t) has no closed form, V(t) also cannot be expressed in closed form.

✓ Closed-form solution obtained
EQ 03 — Water Content (Y)

Y(t) is the only equation that decouples from the system. dsolve returned an exact closed-form solution.

Implicit square-root integral
EQ 04 — Slick Area (A)

dsolve returned an implicit form involving an integral of V(t). Since V(t) is unknown analytically, A(t) cannot be expressed in closed form either.

Exponential integral — all variables coupled
EQ 05 — Viscosity (μ)

dsolve returned an exponential integral expression involving both FE(t) and Y(t). Since FE(t) has no closed form, μ(t) also cannot be expressed analytically.

Key Finding: A complete analytical solution for the coupled system cannot be obtained. MATLAB's dsolve only returned a closed-form solution for the water-content equation (EQ 03). The remaining equations are returned in implicit or integral form due to the nonlinear coupling between the governing variables. Even at equilibrium (all derivatives set to zero), the resulting algebraic system remains implicitly dependent: FE depends on A, A depends on V, and V depends on FE. This confirms that numerical methods are the only viable route to a complete solution.

Numerical Implementation

Three Solvers, One System

Because the coupled ODE system has no complete analytical solution, three numerical methods are implemented and compared. All five state variables are advanced simultaneously at each time step.

Method 01
Euler Method

The simplest first-order explicit scheme. Advances the solution one step using only the derivative at the current point. Works pretty fast but accumulates error quickly, especially for stiff systems like ours where variables change rapidly at t = 0.


% 1. Initial Conditions
FE0 = 0; V0 = 1; Y0 = 0; A0 = 1; mu0 = 1;
x0 = [FE0; V0; Y0; A0; mu0]; 
dt = 0.25;
t  = 0:dt:10;

% 2. Setup storage matrix
xE = zeros(5, length(t));
xE(:,1) = x0; % Set the starting point

% 3. Euler Iteration
for i = 1:length(t)-1
    dx = oilspill(t(i), xE(:,i));
    xE(:,i+1) = xE(:,i) + dt*dx;
end
Method 02
Runge–Kutta (RK4)

Fourth-order accuracy by sampling four derivative estimates (k₁–k₄) at each step. Balances accuracy and efficiency. Adaptive step sizing used: smaller steps early when variation is high, larger steps later.


% 1. Initial Conditions & Setup
FE0 = 0; V0 = 1; Y0 = 0; A0 = 1; mu0 = 1;
x0 = [FE0; V0; Y0; A0; mu0]; 
dt = 0.25;
t  = 0:dt:10;

% 2. Setup storage matrix
xR = zeros(5, length(t));
xR(:,1) = x0; 

% 3. RK4 Iteration
for i = 1:length(t)-1
    k1 = oilspill(t(i), xR(:,i));
    k2 = oilspill(t(i) + dt/2, xR(:,i) + k1*dt/2);
    k3 = oilspill(t(i) + dt/2, xR(:,i) + k2*dt/2);
    k4 = oilspill(t(i) + dt, xR(:,i) + k3*dt);
    
    % Weighted average of slopes
    xR(:,i+1) = xR(:,i) + (dt/6)*(k1 + 2*k2 + 2*k3 + k4);
end
Method 03
MATLAB ode45

MATLAB's built-in Dormand–Prince adaptive RK(4,5) solver. Uses automatic error control and variable step sizing. Serves as the reference benchmark against which Euler and manual RK4 accuracy is evaluated. Available only in MATLAB environment.


% 1. Initial Conditions
FE0 = 0; V0 = 1; Y0 = 0; A0 = 1; mu0 = 1;
x0 = [FE0; V0; Y0; A0; mu0]; 

% 2. Solve using ODE45
% The solver automatically determines the necessary time steps
% to maintain a set tolerance level.
[t, x] = ode45(@oilspill, [0 10], x0);

% 3. 'x' now contains the solution trajectory for all 5 variables 
% across the time vector 't' returned by the solver.

Conclusions

What We Found

The project demonstrates that a coupled ODE system with four-stage Runge–Kutta integration is an effective approach to simulating oil spill fate. The key findings across modelling, analysis, and validation are summarised below.

Coupled simulation outperforms isolated models

Treating all five processes simultaneously through a shared ODE system captures interdependencies (e.g., evaporation reducing surface area that then feeds back into the spreading rate) that are missed when each process is computed separately.

Analytical solution is not achievable for all but one of the ODEs

MATLAB's dsolve confirmed that only the water-content equation (EQ 03) has a closed-form solution. The remaining four equations cannot be decoupled due to nonlinear coupling, exponential terms, and fractional-power expressions. Numerical methods are essential.

RK4 significantly outperforms Euler

In the stiff early-time region (t < 5 h), Euler requires very small step sizes to maintain stability, while RK4 with adaptive stepping maintains accuracy efficiently throughout. So RK4 for the most part is more accurate
