Problem Definition

Marine oil spills represent catastrophic environmental and economic hazards. To effectively respond, we must understand and predict how oil behaves once released into the ocean.

The behavior of an oil spill is governed by a complex set of physical and chemical processes collectively known as weathering. These processes occur simultaneously and interact with one another, strongly dependent on environmental conditions such as wind speed, sea surface temperature, and wave energy.

Weathering Processes Modeled

Spreading

The initial physical expansion of the oil slick over the water surface, driven by gravity and surface tension, resisted by inertia and viscosity. Dominant in the first few hours.

Diagram for spreading
Evaporation

The mass transfer of lighter, volatile hydrocarbon fractions into the atmosphere. This drastically alters the volume and properties of the remaining oil.

Diagram for evaporation
Emulsification (Water Incorporation)

The formation of a "mousse" (water-in-oil emulsion) induced by wave action. This significantly increases the volume and viscosity of the slick.

Diagram for emulsification
Natural Dispersion

The entrainment of oil droplets into the water column due to breaking waves. Only smaller droplets remain permanently dispersed.

Diagram for natural dispersion
Viscosity & Density Changes

Consequential changes to the physical properties of the oil as lighter fractions evaporate and water is incorporated, making the slick thicker and heavier.
